Nutrition Foundations

▼

Why Nutrition Is Everything

Training is the stimulus. Nutrition is the raw material. Without adequate nutrition — particularly protein and calories — training stimulus produces no adaptation. You can have the perfect programme and produce zero results with poor nutrition. Conversely, good nutrition alone without training produces fat loss but also significant muscle loss. The combination of training and nutrition is where transformation happens.

Energy Balance

The fundamental law of body composition: Calories In vs Calories Out (CICO). To lose fat: consume fewer calories than you expend (deficit). To gain muscle: consume more than you expend (surplus). To maintain: match intake to expenditure. Everything in nutrition works within this framework — no diet "breaks" the laws of thermodynamics.

Macronutrients

The three main energy-providing nutrients: Protein (4 kcal/g), Carbohydrates (4 kcal/g), and Fat (9 kcal/g). Each plays distinct roles beyond energy. Micronutrients (vitamins, minerals) provide no energy but are essential for function. Alcohol provides 7 kcal/g with minimal nutritional benefit.

Adherence Is King

The best diet is the one a person can consistently stick to. A "perfect" plan followed 50% of the time produces worse results than a "good" plan followed 100% of the time. Sustainability, enjoyment, and flexibility are not weaknesses in a nutrition plan — they are the most important success factors.

How to Work Out Your Macros — Step by Step

1

Calculate Your TDEE

Use the Mifflin-St Jeor formula above or use the calculator. This is your starting calorie estimate. Remember: it's an estimate. Real-world tracking over 2–3 weeks will tell you your true TDEE more accurately than any formula.

2

Set Your Calorie Target

Apply your goal deficit or surplus to your TDEE. Fat loss: TDEE − 300–500 kcal. Muscle gain: TDEE + 200–400 kcal. Maintenance: TDEE. This is your daily calorie target.

3

Set Protein First

Set protein at 1.8–2.2g per kg of body weight for most active people. Calculate calories from protein: protein grams × 4. Example: 80kg person → 160g protein → 640 kcal from protein.

4

Set Fat Minimum

Set fat at minimum 0.8–1g per kg of body weight (never below 0.5g/kg). Calculate calories from fat: fat grams × 9. Example: 80kg person → 70g fat → 630 kcal from fat.

5

Fill Remaining Calories with Carbohydrates

Remaining calories ÷ 4 = grams of carbohydrate. Example: 2000 kcal target − 640 (protein) − 630 (fat) = 730 kcal remaining ÷ 4 = 182g carbohydrate.

6

Verify & Adjust

Add all calories: (protein g × 4) + (carb g × 4) + (fat g × 9) = total daily calories. This should equal your target. Adjust carbs up or down to hit your exact calorie target.

7

Track for 2–3 Weeks, Then Adjust

Weigh yourself daily (first thing, after toilet, before food or drink). Average weight across each week. If weekly average is not moving in the right direction after 2 weeks, adjust calories by 100–200 kcal/day and reassess. The formula gives you a starting point — your body gives you the truth.

Calories — How to Calculate & Apply

▼

Total Daily Energy Expenditure (TDEE)

TDEE is the total number of calories you burn in a day. It is composed of four components. Understanding each helps you accurately estimate intake needs for any client.

BMR
~60–70% of TDEE
NEAT
~15–30% of TDEE
TEF
~8–15% of TDEE
EEE
~5–15% of TDEE

BMR — Basal Metabolic Rate

The number of calories your body burns at complete rest — just to maintain organ function, body temperature, and basic cellular processes. The largest component of TDEE (60–70%). Influenced by lean muscle mass (more muscle = higher BMR), age (declines ~2% per decade after 30), sex (males generally higher due to more muscle), and genetics.

NEAT — Non-Exercise Activity Thermogenesis

Calories burned through all movement that is NOT formal exercise: walking, fidgeting, standing, stairs, household tasks. Highly variable between individuals — a busy job or active lifestyle can mean NEAT of 1000+ kcal/day vs 200 kcal/day for a sedentary office worker. The most underestimated and variable component of TDEE. Dramatically reduced during caloric restriction (the body moves less without conscious effort).

TEF — Thermic Effect of Food

The calories burned digesting, absorbing, and processing food. Protein has the highest TEF at 20–30% of its calories (eat 100 kcal of protein, net 70–80 kcal). Carbohydrates: 5–10%. Fat: 0–3%. This is one reason high-protein diets aid fat loss beyond just satiety effects — protein literally costs more energy to digest.

EEE — Exercise Energy Expenditure

Calories burned during structured exercise sessions. Most people overestimate this significantly. A 60-minute gym session burns approximately 200–400 kcal for most individuals — less than a chocolate bar for many. Cardio burns more per session than weights. Weights build muscle which raises BMR long-term. Wearables overestimate exercise calories by 20–93% depending on the device.

The Mifflin-St Jeor BMR Formula

The most accurate widely-used BMR formula for most populations:

Male BMR: (10 × weight in kg) + (6.25 × height in cm) − (5 × age) + 5

Female BMR: (10 × weight in kg) + (6.25 × height in cm) − (5 × age) − 161

Multiply BMR by an activity multiplier to get TDEE:

TDEE calculators give a starting estimate — real-world tracking and body weight trends over 2–3 weeks are the most accurate TDEE assessment.

Protein is the most important macronutrient for physique goals. It is the only macronutrient that directly contributes to building and repairing muscle tissue. It is also the most satiating macronutrient — significantly reducing hunger when intake is adequate. High protein diets are associated with superior body composition outcomes regardless of total calorie level.

Muscle Protein Synthesis (MPS): The process of building new muscle proteins. Triggered by resistance training and dietary protein. Protein must provide sufficient leucine (~3g per meal) to maximally stimulate MPS — this equates to approximately 30–40g of most complete protein sources per meal.

Protein distribution: MPS is maximally stimulated per meal and then returns to baseline. Distributing protein across 4–6 meals/day with 30–40g per serving stimulates MPS more frequently than eating all protein in 1–2 large meals. Total daily intake matters most, but distribution matters for optimising anabolic signalling.

Complete vs incomplete proteins: Complete proteins contain all 9 essential amino acids. Animal proteins (meat, fish, eggs, dairy) are complete. Most plant proteins are incomplete (lacking one or more essential amino acids) but can be combined to form complete profiles (rice + beans, for example).

High protein food sources: Chicken breast (31g/100g), tuna (29g/100g), egg whites (11g/100g), Greek yoghurt (10g/100g), cottage cheese (11g/100g), salmon (25g/100g), lean beef (26g/100g), tofu (8g/100g), tempeh (19g/100g), lentils (9g/100g cooked).

Carbohydrates are the primary fuel source for high-intensity exercise and the preferred fuel for the brain. Stored as glycogen in muscle (300–400g) and liver (80–100g). Glycogen depletion is the primary cause of fatigue in intense training — carbohydrates directly fuel the sessions that build muscle and burn fat.

Simple vs Complex: Simple carbohydrates (sugars) are digested and absorbed rapidly — useful peri-workout (around training). Complex carbohydrates (starches, fibre) are digested slowly — provide sustained energy and superior satiety for most of the day.

Fibre: A form of carbohydrate that is not digested and absorbed. Essential for gut microbiome health, satiety, blood glucose regulation, and cardiovascular health. Target: 25–35g per day. Sources: vegetables, fruits, legumes, wholegrains, oats. Most clients are significantly under-consuming fibre — increasing it dramatically improves satiety on a diet.

Glycaemic Index (GI): How rapidly a food raises blood glucose. High GI foods (white rice, sugary drinks) spike blood glucose rapidly. Low GI foods (oats, sweet potato, legumes) provide gradual release. For most people's goals, the GI of individual foods matters far less than total carbohydrate quality and quantity over the day.

Good carb sources: Oats, sweet potato, white/brown rice, wholegrain bread, pasta, fruit, vegetables, legumes (lentils, chickpeas, black beans), quinoa.

Dietary fats are essential for hormone production (testosterone, oestrogen, cortisol), fat-soluble vitamin absorption (A, D, E, K), cell membrane integrity, and long-duration low-intensity energy. Fat intake should never drop below 0.5g/kg body weight — severe fat restriction impairs testosterone production, hormonal function, and micronutrient absorption.

Saturated fats: Found in animal products (meat, dairy) and tropical oils (coconut oil). Should comprise no more than 10% of total calories for cardiovascular health. Not inherently harmful in moderate amounts — excessive intake is associated with elevated LDL cholesterol.

Unsaturated fats (monounsaturated and polyunsaturated): Found in olive oil, avocados, nuts, fatty fish. Actively beneficial for cardiovascular health, inflammation, and cognitive function. Omega-3 fatty acids (EPA/DHA from fatty fish; ALA from flaxseed) are the most important — most people are significantly omega-3 deficient. Anti-inflammatory, beneficial for lipids, brain health, and joint health.

Trans fats: Artificially hydrogenated fats found in processed foods and many takeaways. The only truly "bad" fat — no safe level of consumption. Significantly raises LDL while lowering HDL. Largely removed from most food supplies due to regulation but still present in some processed products.

Good fat sources: Avocado, olive oil, nuts (almonds, walnuts, cashews), oily fish (salmon, mackerel, sardines), eggs, chia seeds, flaxseeds, full-fat dairy (in moderation).

Macro Tracking — How To Do It

▼

Why Track?

Most people have absolutely no idea how much they eat. Studies consistently show people underestimate their caloric intake by 20–47%. Tracking — even for 4–8 weeks — builds a permanent understanding of portion sizes, calorie density, and macronutrient content that improves food choices for life. It doesn't have to be forever, but it needs to happen long enough to build accurate intuition.

Step 1 — Get a Food Scale

A food scale is non-negotiable for accurate tracking. Volume measurements (cups, tablespoons) are wildly inaccurate — a cup of oats can vary by 30–50% depending on how it's packed. Weigh everything in grams. £10–15 from any supermarket. The single most impactful nutrition tool a client can buy. Weight food raw before cooking where possible — cooking changes the weight of foods significantly.

Step 2 — Use a Tracking App

MyFitnessPal: Largest food database. Free version sufficient. Scan barcodes for packaged foods. Most widely used.

Cronometer: More accurate micronutrient data. Better for clients focused on overall nutrition quality, not just macros.

Nutrific / Lose It: Simpler interfaces — better for clients who find MyFitnessPal overwhelming. The best app is the one the client will consistently use.

How to Log Food Accurately

1

Log Everything — No Exceptions

Cooking oil, sauces, butter, drinks, condiments, "tasting" while cooking — all must be logged. These "invisible" calories account for hundreds of kcal per day for many people and are the most common reason for a calorie budget not working. "If it passes your lips, log it."

2

Search the Correct Entry

MyFitnessPal's user-submitted database contains many inaccurate entries. For whole foods (chicken breast, oats, eggs), use entries marked "verified" or cross-reference with the nutritional label. For packaged foods, scan the barcode — this pulls the manufacturer's data directly.

3

Pre-Log Where Possible

Log the next day's food the night before. This allows you to see your macro balance before you eat and adjust accordingly — rather than discovering you've blown your protein or calories at the end of the day when it's too late to change anything. Planning ahead is the most powerful tracking habit.

4

Eating Out — How to Estimate

Most major chains have calorie information on their menus or apps — use these. For independent restaurants, make your best estimate using similar dishes from your tracking history. Overestimate slightly. Order simple dishes where ingredients are clear (grilled protein + vegetables + a carb source). Sauces and dressings are the hidden calorie bombs at restaurants.

5

Consistency Over Perfection

Tracking 90% accurately, consistently, produces far better results than tracking perfectly for 3 weeks then stopping. If you miss a meal log — don't give up the day. Log what you can remember and continue. One bad day of logging does not ruin progress. Stopping entirely does.

Common Tracking Mistakes

Logging cooked weights as raw (or vice versa): 100g of raw chicken breast is not the same as 100g of cooked. Raw is always heavier than cooked (water evaporates). Always use the same reference — raw is the most consistent. Not logging liquids: Milk, fruit juice, protein shakes, alcohol, and even certain herbal drinks contain significant calories. Forgetting meal prep oils: 1 tablespoon of olive oil = ~120 kcal. It's invisible but significant. Assuming "healthy" means "low calorie": Avocado, nuts, olive oil, and oat milk are nutritious but calorically dense. A handful of almonds is ~170 kcal.

Carbohydrates — Quality, Timing & Sources

▼

Pre-Workout Carbohydrates

Carbohydrates are the primary fuel for high-intensity resistance training. Glycogen availability directly impacts training performance — low glycogen reduces strength, power output, and endurance. Strategic carb intake around training maximises session quality.

2–4 hours pre-workout: Full mixed meal with complex carbs (rice, oats, potato, pasta) — 1–2g/kg body weight.

30–60 minutes pre-workout: Small, quickly digestible option — banana, rice cake, fruit, or a small serving of oats.

Training fasted: Acceptable for lower-intensity sessions. For heavy compound work or high-volume training, performance will be compromised without carbohydrate availability.

Post-Workout Carbohydrates

After training, muscle glycogen is partially depleted and the window for glycogen resynthesis is enhanced (GLUT-4 transporter expression is elevated). Consuming carbohydrates post-workout accelerates glycogen replenishment.

Practical importance: If training once daily with 24+ hours until next session, the glycogen replenishment window is less critical — total daily carbohydrate intake matters more than the specific window. For twice-daily training or sessions within 8 hours, post-workout carbohydrate timing becomes significant.

Post-workout carbs: 0.5–1g/kg body weight within 2 hours of training, combined with protein (30–40g) for maximum glycogen and MPS response.

Why Sodium Matters More Than Most People Think

Sodium is the most important extracellular electrolyte in the human body. It controls fluid balance, nerve transmission, muscle contraction, blood pressure, and nutrient absorption. For athletes especially, sodium is not the enemy it's been made out to be — understanding when you need more, when you need less, and where to get it is essential for performance, health, and body composition.

Converting Between mg and g

1g sodium = 1,000mg sodium — simple.

The daily WHO limit of 2,300mg = 2.3g sodium

But most food labels show salt (NaCl), not sodium. Salt and sodium are not the same:

1g salt = 0.4g sodium (400mg sodium)

1g sodium = 2.5g salt

So a product saying "1.5g salt per portion" contains 0.6g (600mg) sodium — divide salt by 2.5 to get sodium.

Quick conversions:
500mg sodium = 0.5g = 1.25g salt
1,000mg sodium = 1.0g = 2.5g salt
2,300mg sodium = 2.3g = 5.75g salt
4,000mg sodium = 4.0g = 10.0g salt

How Much Is Lost in Sweat?

Average sweat sodium concentration: 500–1,500mg per litre of sweat

Typical sweat rate: 0.5–2.5 litres per hour depending on intensity, heat, and individual.

Sodium loss per hour of exercise:

Light exercise: ~200–500mg (0.2–0.5g) per hour

Moderate exercise: ~500–1,000mg (0.5–1.0g) per hour

Intense exercise in heat: ~1,000–3,000mg (1.0–3.0g) per hour

Why this matters: A 90-minute football match in summer can cost a player 2–4g of sodium through sweat alone. Without replacement, blood sodium drops — causing fatigue, cramping, headache, and in extreme cases, hyponatraemia (dangerous low blood sodium).

Sweat test: Weigh yourself before and after a 60-min session without drinking. Every kg lost is approximately 1 litre of sweat. Multiply by your sweat sodium concentration (estimated 900mg/L average) to get approximate sodium loss.

⚡ Sodium Timing — Before, During & After Exercise
Getting sodium in at the right time is as important as how much
▼

Pre-Exercise Sodium Loading

Why load sodium before exercise? Sodium pre-loading expands plasma volume — more blood volume means better oxygen delivery, lower heart rate at the same effort, and reduced dehydration rate during exercise.

Protocol: 1,000–1,500mg (1.0–1.5g) sodium in the 2 hours before an endurance event or very long session. Drink 500ml of fluid alongside.

Practical sources: Electrolyte drink with 500–1,000mg sodium, salted porridge, eggs on toast with a pinch of salt, or a dedicated electrolyte pre-load product (Precision Hydration 1500 tab).

When is pre-loading most useful? Endurance events over 90 minutes, hot weather training, or when you know you are a heavy sweater. For a 45-minute gym session — unnecessary.

During Exercise

Under 60 minutes: Water is sufficient for most people. Sodium replacement unnecessary for short sessions.

60–90 minutes: 200–500mg (0.2–0.5g) sodium per hour via isotonic drink or electrolyte tab. Prevents early decline in blood sodium.

90 minutes–3 hours: 500–1,000mg (0.5–1.0g) per hour. Electrolyte drinks or tabs. Match drinking rate to sweat rate.

Over 3 hours (ultra-endurance): 1,000–1,500mg (1.0–1.5g) per hour or more for very high sweaters. Real food with sodium (salted nuts, crisps, savoury gels) can supplement drink-based electrolytes.

The hyponatraemia warning: Drinking large volumes of plain water during long events without sodium dilutes blood sodium — causing nausea, headache, confusion, and in severe cases, death. Never drink only plain water during events over 2 hours.

Post-Exercise Sodium Recovery

Replace lost sodium as a priority alongside fluid. Drinking plain water post-exercise without sodium doesn't restore fluid balance — the kidneys excrete the excess water because blood sodium remains low, creating a cycle of drinking and still feeling thirsty.

Post-exercise protocol: Electrolyte drink containing 500–1,000mg sodium per 500ml. For every kg of body weight lost during exercise — drink 1.5 litres of electrolyte fluid. This accounts for ongoing urinary and sweat losses during recovery.

Food-based recovery (preferred for most): A post-exercise meal containing natural sodium sources achieves rehydration and refuelling simultaneously. Chicken and rice cooked with a pinch of salt, a protein shake with milk, or a cheese and egg meal all provide sodium alongside carbohydrate and protein.

Combat sport rehydration: After a weight cut, sodium is critical. 1,500–2,000mg (1.5–2.0g) sodium in the immediate post-weigh-in electrolyte drink prevents hyponatraemia from the rapid rehydration. Never rehydrate post-cut with plain water alone.

🩺 Sodium & Health — Blood Pressure, Kidney & Heart
The evidence on sodium and health — more nuanced than the headlines suggest
▼

Sodium and Blood Pressure

The evidence: In salt-sensitive individuals (approximately 25–30% of the population), high sodium intake raises blood pressure meaningfully. In the remaining 70–75%, sodium has a much smaller effect on blood pressure than commonly believed.

Who is salt-sensitive? Older adults, those with existing hypertension, people of African heritage, and those with kidney disease are more likely to be salt-sensitive and benefit most from sodium restriction.

For fit, active adults: The blood pressure risk from moderate sodium intake is substantially offset by regular exercise, adequate potassium intake, and healthy body weight. These factors matter more than sodium restriction alone for blood pressure management in athletes.

The DASH diet approach: Rather than focusing solely on reducing sodium, increasing potassium (from fruits and vegetables) is equally or more important for blood pressure management. The ratio of sodium to potassium matters as much as the absolute sodium level.

Too Little Sodium — The Risks

Hyponatraemia (low blood sodium): Blood sodium below 135mmol/L. Causes nausea, headache, confusion, and in severe cases (<125mmol/L) — seizures, coma, and death. More common than most people think in endurance athletes who drink too much plain water.

Chronic mild sodium restriction in athletes: Muscle cramping, fatigue, dizziness on standing (orthostatic hypotension), reduced training performance, and impaired cognitive function. Athletes who aggressively restrict sodium to "reduce water retention" often feel worse, not better.

Hormonal effects: Low sodium triggers aldosterone and renin release — the body's sodium-conservation system. Chronic activation of this system can actually raise blood pressure in some individuals — the paradox of aggressive sodium restriction backfiring.

The bottom line: For healthy, active people — the risk of too little sodium is as real as the risk of too much. The WHO targets apply to sedentary adults. Athletes need more, not less.

Does Meal Timing Matter?

Total daily calories and macronutrients are significantly more important than meal timing for body composition. However, strategic timing can optimise performance, recovery, and satiety — particularly for trained athletes. "Meal timing matters, but total intake matters more."

Meal Frequency

3–6 meals per day is optimal for most people. Each meal should ideally contain 30–40g protein to maximally stimulate MPS. More frequent meals stimulate MPS more times per day — but this matters significantly less than total daily protein intake.

Intermittent fasting (16:8, 18:6) works for fat loss not because fasting has magic properties but because it naturally restricts the eating window and reduces total calorie intake. MPS is not meaningfully impacted by fasting windows in the research — total daily protein is what matters.

Peri-Workout Nutrition

Pre-workout (2–4 hours before): Full meal — protein + carbs + low fat/fibre (to avoid GI discomfort during training).

Pre-workout (30–60 min before): Small, fast-digesting option if needed — banana, rice cake with protein powder.

Post-workout (within 1–2 hours): Protein (30–40g) + carbohydrates (0.5–1g/kg). The "anabolic window" is much wider than once believed — hitting protein targets over the full day matters more than a strict 30-minute post-workout window. But getting protein in within 2 hours is still good practice.

Nutrition in Sport — Before, During & After

▼

Why Sport Nutrition Is Different

General nutrition rules apply to everyone. Sport nutrition is about timing and precision on top of those rules. The goal is simple: arrive at every session fuelled, recover from every session fast, and perform at your best when it matters — on matchday or race day. Get this right and training quality improves, recovery time drops, and performance on the day is measurably better.

Night Before — Game, Race or Key Session
Glycogen loading window
▼

The night before a game, race, or important training session is your primary glycogen loading opportunity. Muscle glycogen is stored carbohydrate — it is the direct fuel source for high-intensity exercise. Starting an event with full glycogen stores can mean the difference between strong play in the final 20 minutes and running on empty.

The goal: Maximise muscle and liver glycogen stores without leaving the stomach heavy or uncomfortable the following morning.

What to Eat

High carbohydrate: Aim for 6–10g/kg body weight of carbohydrate across the full day. For a 75kg person that's 450–750g of carbs — substantially more than a normal day.

Moderate protein: 1.6–2g/kg — maintain your usual protein but don't go heavy. Large protein portions slow gastric emptying.

Low fat and low fibre: Both slow digestion and can cause GI discomfort during performance. Avoid high-fat meals, heavy sauces, and excessive raw vegetables the night before.

Easy to digest carb sources: White rice, pasta, white bread, potato (boiled or mashed), oats, banana, fruit juice. Avoid wholegrain heavy options the night before — the extra fibre is not your friend.

Example evening meal: Large portion of pasta (200g dry weight) with a lean tomato and chicken sauce. Low in fat, high in carbs, easy on the gut. Follow with banana and a glass of fruit juice if still hungry.

What to Avoid

High fat meals: Takeaway, fried food, heavy cream sauces, cheese-heavy dishes. Fat slows gastric emptying — you'll wake up still full and heavy.

New or unusual foods: Never experiment the night before performance. Stick to foods you know your gut handles well.

Excess alcohol: Impairs glycogen synthesis, disrupts sleep architecture, dehydrates, and impairs reaction time and decision-making the next day. Even one or two drinks the night before competition will reduce performance.

Excessive fibre: Beans, lentils, large amounts of raw vegetables, bran cereals. All increase risk of GI distress during performance.

Large late-night portion: Eat your main carb meal 3–4 hours before your intended sleep time so digestion is largely complete before bed.

Sleep — The Most Underrated Night-Before Strategy

7–9 hours of quality sleep is more valuable than any pre-match meal. GH is released during deep sleep, reaction time is directly linked to sleep quality, and glycogen synthesis continues overnight when combined with adequate carbohydrate intake. Prioritise sleep over a perfect meal every time.

Never try anything new on matchday or race day. Every pre-event nutrition strategy should be practised in training first. Your gut needs rehearsing just like your legs do. If you want to use gels on race day — practice with them on long training runs first.

For events over 60–90 minutes: carbohydrate delivery becomes critical. Glycogen stores begin to deplete meaningfully after 60–75 minutes of sustained high-intensity effort. Without replacement, performance drops, pace slows, and decision-making deteriorates.

Hydration During Performance

Aim to drink 400–800ml per hour during endurance events, adjusted for conditions (temperature, sweat rate, intensity). Half time (typically 10–15 min) is a specific fuelling window unique to team sports. Research shows half-time carbohydrate intake significantly improves second-half performance compared to water alone.

Half-time protocol: Isotonic sports drink (500ml) as soon as you stop. Orange segments, banana, or energy gel within the first 5 minutes. Avoid heavy or unfamiliar foods. Rehydrate based on sweat lost — drink to thirst. Some elite teams use caffeine chewing gum at half time for the second-half CNS boost.

This is the window that matters most for recovery speed.

Once the immediate recovery window is covered, eat a full mixed meal within 1–2 hours. This is where you rebuild properly.

High protein: 30–50g from lean meat, fish, eggs, or dairy. This drives sustained muscle protein synthesis.

High carbohydrate: Rice, pasta, potato, bread — restore glycogen fully before the next session.

Vegetables: Antioxidants and micronutrients from colourful veg support the inflammatory response and cellular repair process.

Anti-inflammatory additions: Tart cherry juice (500ml), omega-3 from oily fish or supplement, turmeric in cooking. All have evidence for reducing muscle soreness and speeding recovery markers.

When Recovery Nutrition Is Most Critical

If you play/train again within 24 hours — recovery nutrition is non-negotiable. Every hour of delay in consuming protein and carbs post-event is an hour of suboptimal recovery. If you have 48+ hours before the next session, the urgency of the immediate window is reduced but still beneficial. For once-weekly sport players, total weekly nutrition matters more than the precise post-match window — though getting into the habit of good recovery nutrition is worth building regardless.

Sport-Specific Nutrition Plans

▼

Why Each Sport Is Different

A marathon runner and a golfer both need good nutrition — but the demands, timing, duration, and intensity differ completely. What works pre-match for a rugby forward could leave a golfer heavy and sluggish. These plans are built around the real physiological demands of each sport — use them as exact protocols for clients competing in these disciplines.

⚽ Football
High-intensity intermittent · 90 min · 10–14km covered
▼

Physiological Demands

Football is the ultimate test of intermittent high-intensity exercise. Players cover 10–14km per match with 200–400m of maximal sprinting, 2–3km of high-intensity running, and over 1,000 changes of direction. Glycogen is the primary fuel — studies show muscle glycogen can drop by 40–90% during a 90-minute match. Players who run low on glycogen in the second half lose sprint speed, decision-making, and passing accuracy. Nutrition is literally the difference between performance in the 85th minute and fatigue.

🌙 Night Before Matchday

Target: 7–8g/kg body weight carbohydrate across the full day.

Evening meal (3–4 hours before sleep): Large bowl of pasta or white rice with a lean protein source (chicken breast, turkey, or white fish). Tomato-based sauce — not cream. Side of easily digestible vegetables (courgette, green beans — not broccoli or sprouts which cause bloating). White bread roll. Glass of fruit juice or sports drink.

Optional pre-sleep snack: Banana + glass of milk or yoghurt. This tops up liver glycogen that depletes overnight while sleeping.

Avoid: High-fat meal (takeaway, fried food), large quantities of raw veg or beans, alcohol, unfamiliar foods, heavy spice.

Hydration: Sip 500–750ml of fluid during the evening. Aim to wake up with clear-pale urine.

🍳 Matchday Morning (3–4 hrs before)

Target: 2–3g/kg carbohydrate. Low fat, low fibre, moderate protein.

Ideal pre-match meal: Porridge (oats) with banana and honey, or white toast with scrambled eggs and baked beans, or pasta/rice (smaller portion than night before) with chicken. Keep it familiar — this is not the day to try something new.

Protein: 20–30g — enough to support muscle function without slowing gastric emptying.

Timing matters: If kick-off is at 3pm, eat by 11–11:30am. If evening kick-off (7:30pm), eat at 3:30–4pm then a small top-up snack at 6pm.

Coffee: Caffeine 3–6mg/kg 45–60 min before kick-off significantly improves sprint performance, reaction time, and decision-making. This is evidence-backed. Time it to peak during warm-up.

🍌 45–60 Minutes Before Kick-Off

Light carb top-up: Banana, energy gel, 500ml isotonic sports drink, a few dates, or a small energy bar. Keep it simple and single-ingredient where possible.

Do not: Eat a full meal this close to kick-off. Blood gets diverted to the gut for digestion — pulling it away from muscles. GI cramping mid-first-half is the result.

Fluids: 300–500ml water or isotonic drink in the final hour. Check urine colour — pale yellow is your target.

⚡ Half-Time (15 minutes)

This window is critical: Research consistently shows half-time carbohydrate intake improves second-half sprint performance, distance covered, and technical execution compared to water alone.

Half-time protocol: Immediately on sitting down — 500ml isotonic sports drink. Within 5 minutes: banana, orange segments, or energy gel. Some teams use caffeine chewing gum (200mg) at half-time for a second-half CNS boost — the rapid buccal absorption means it peaks 15–20 minutes into the second half exactly when you need it most.

Avoid: Heavy foods, excessive volume, anything that hasn't been practised before.

🔄 Post-Match Recovery (the 30-minute window)

Glycogen resynthesis is rapid for 30–60 minutes post-match — GLUT-4 transporters are maximally active. Don't waste this window.

Immediate (0–30 min): 500ml chocolate milk OR a recovery shake (30–40g protein + 60–80g carbs). Chocolate milk has an almost perfect 3:1–4:1 carb:protein ratio and is as effective as commercial recovery products. Rehydrate — drink 1.5× the fluid weight lost during the match.

Post-match meal (1–2 hrs): High carbohydrate + high protein. Rice or pasta + chicken/beef/fish + colourful vegetables. Add tart cherry juice (500ml) — strong evidence for reducing muscle soreness and improving recovery speed between training sessions and games.

If playing again within 48 hours: Treat every meal post-match as a recovery meal. Priority is glycogen replenishment — keep carbs high (6–8g/kg/day) across the full 48-hour period. Protein at 2.0–2.4g/kg throughout.

⛳ Golf
4–5 hours · 12–15km walking · Sustained cognitive precision
▼

Physiological Demands

Golf is often misunderstood as "non-athletic" — but 18 holes involves 4–5 hours of sustained activity, 12–15km of walking (more carrying a bag), hundreds of precise motor outputs, and sustained high-level decision-making and focus throughout. The primary nutritional demands in golf are not glycogen-based like team sports — they are blood glucose stability for sustained cognitive function, and sustained energy for motor precision. A drop in blood glucose in the back nine causes loss of focus, poorer decision-making, club selection errors, and mechanical swing breakdown. Nutrition in golf is about maintaining the brain as much as the body.

🌙 Night Before Round

Target: 4–5g/kg carbohydrate — less than team sports as the intensity is lower, but consistent energy is still key.

Evening meal: Balanced mixed meal — carbohydrate base (pasta, rice, potato), lean protein (chicken, fish), plenty of vegetables. No need for aggressive carb loading. Focus is on a good-quality meal with adequate carbs and protein rather than volume.

Alcohol: Golf and social drinking often go together — but even 1–2 drinks the night before a competitive round impairs fine motor control, reaction time, decision-making, and sleep quality. If playing competitively, treat it like any other performance sport.

Sleep: Particularly important in golf. Reaction time, fine motor control, and decision-making — all critical golf skills — are directly linked to sleep quality. 8 hours is not a luxury in the night before competition.

🍳 Pre-Round Meal (2–3 hrs before tee time)

Target: 1.5–2g/kg carbohydrate, moderate protein, low fat.

Ideal meals: Porridge with banana and honey — the gold standard for golf. Sustained energy release, easy on the stomach, stabilises blood glucose for the first 4–5 holes. Or: wholegrain toast + eggs + fruit juice. Or: bagel with peanut butter + banana.

Avoid: A heavy fry-up or full English — high fat slows digestion and causes sluggishness through holes 1–6. Large dairy or high fibre foods that cause GI discomfort. Too much caffeine on an empty stomach which causes jitters that destroy putting touch.

Caffeine: Use carefully in golf. 2–3mg/kg is enough for cognitive benefit without the jitters that destroy fine motor control. Coffee before a round enhances focus and reaction time, but excess caffeine causes hand tremor — disastrous for putting. Avoid on an empty stomach.

⛳ On the Course — During Round

This is where golf nutrition differs most from other sports. There is no half-time, no substitution break, and often limited access to food for hours at a time. Blood glucose management across 4–5 hours is the key skill.

Every 4–5 holes (roughly every 45–60 min): Small carbohydrate top-up. Banana, handful of dates, energy bar, cereal bar, fruit, or sports drink. The goal is preventing the blood glucose dip that causes "back-nine brain" — the loss of focus and decision-making quality in holes 13–18.

Carry in your bag: Bananas, dates or dried fruit, cereal bars or energy bars, nuts and raisins mix, water, sports drink. Simple, portable, easy to eat between shots.

Hydration: Golf is often played in warm weather over 4–5 hours. Dehydration impairs fine motor control and cognitive function at just 1–2% body weight loss. Drink 300–400ml per hour minimum. Use the time walking between shots to drink regularly.

Avoid the 19th hole mentality mid-round: Stopping for a large lunch between nines causes a post-meal blood glucose spike then crash — exactly the mechanism behind poor back-nine performance. A light snack beats a full meal mid-round.

🔄 Post-Round Recovery

Golf does not cause the same acute muscle damage as contact or high-intensity sports — but 4–5 hours of walking with a bag, hundreds of rotational swing forces, and sustained focus still depletes glycogen and fatigues the neuromuscular system.

Post-round meal: Mixed protein + carbohydrate meal within 60–90 minutes of finishing. No rush post-round — unlike team sports, there's no re-competition within 24 hours in most cases. A balanced meal rather than an immediate recovery shake is fine.

The 19th hole: Socially important in golf culture. If drinking post-round, eat a proper meal first. The combination of low blood glucose post-round + alcohol significantly amplifies the dehydrating and performance-impairing effects of alcohol.

Tournament play (multiple days): Recovery between rounds matters. High carbohydrate evening meal, adequate protein (1.6–2.0g/kg), prioritise sleep. Anti-inflammatory nutrition (omega-3, tart cherry) supports the accumulated fatigue of multi-day competition.

🏃 Running
5K to Marathon · Duration-dependent nutrition strategy
▼

Physiological Demands — Duration Changes Everything

Running nutrition is uniquely dependent on race distance. A 5K runner has almost zero intra-race nutrition requirement. A marathon runner who gets it wrong will hit the wall. The protocols below cover all distances — apply the one that matches your event. The unifying principles: glycogen is king, hydration is critical, and GI distress is the number one race-day ruiner that can be almost entirely prevented with proper nutrition practice.

🌙 Night Before

5K / 10K: Normal high-carb evening meal. No specific loading required — the race is short enough that starting glycogen levels from a normal diet are sufficient. Focus on a familiar, easy-to-digest meal. Early dinner (6–7pm) to allow full digestion.

Half Marathon: 7–8g/kg carbohydrate. Start carb-loading from lunchtime the day before (not just the evening meal). White rice or pasta + lean protein + easily digestible veg. Limit fibre significantly — the most common cause of race-day GI disasters is too much fibre in the 24 hours before a half marathon.

Marathon: 48-hour carb loading protocol. 8–10g/kg carbohydrate on both the day before and the day before that. Reduce training volume (tapering) so glycogen stores are at their absolute maximum. Eat more rice, pasta, white bread, bananas, fruit juice than feels normal. Aggressively reduce fibre, fat, and unfamiliar foods.

All distances — sleep: Race-day nerves often disrupt sleep. The night before the night before matters as much — prioritise that sleep if you know nerves will keep you up race-eve.

🍳 Race Morning

5K / 10K (sub-60 min effort): Light breakfast 2–3 hours before. Oats, banana, toast with honey. 1–1.5g/kg carbs. Coffee if you use it. Nothing heavy or experimental.

Half Marathon: Full breakfast 3 hours before. Porridge + banana + sports drink. 2–3g/kg carbs. Protein kept to 20–25g — enough to prevent muscle breakdown without slowing digestion. Caffeine 3–6mg/kg, 45–60 min before start.

Marathon: Breakfast 3–4 hours before — the earlier the better given typical early marathon start times. Large bowl of porridge/oats, banana, white toast, sports drink or juice. 3–4g/kg carbs. Energy gel 10–15 minutes before the gun.

GI protocol: Race morning bowel movement is critical for comfort. A morning coffee usually assists this. Allow enough time post-breakfast for a toilet visit before the warm-up.

Pre-hydration: 5–7ml/kg in the 4 hours before. Don't chug water immediately before — spread it across 4 hours. For a 70kg runner: 350–490ml over 4 hours before start.

Never drink solely plain water in events over 2 hours — use electrolyte drinks or take sodium tablets alongside water.

🏋️ HYROX
8km run + 8 stations · 45–90 min · High aerobic + strength combined
▼

Physiological Demands

HYROX combines 8km of running with 8 functional exercise stations in a standardised format — SkiErg, Sled Push, Sled Pull, Burpee Broad Jumps, Rowing, Farmers Carry, Sandbag Lunges, and Wall Balls. Total time for most competitors: 45–90 minutes (open), 45–60 minutes (elite). The unique demand is the combination of aerobic endurance AND muscular strength-endurance — both glycolytic and oxidative energy systems are heavily taxed. The post-station lactate accumulation means glycogen availability going into each run leg is critical. Poor nutrition = your running pace deteriorates, your stations feel heavier, and your grip gives out.

🌙 Night Before

Target: 6–8g/kg carbohydrate. HYROX sits between team sport and endurance race in terms of glycogen demand — treat it like a half marathon from a carb-loading perspective.

Evening meal: Large carbohydrate-focused meal. Rice, pasta, or potato as the base. Lean protein (chicken, turkey, white fish). Low fat, low fibre. Simple preparation — nothing experimental.

Specific HYROX consideration: The sled push and farmers carry have a significant grip and upper body demand that pure running does not. Ensuring adequate creatine levels (if using) matters — creatine supports the ATP-PCr system used in the explosive station efforts.

Hydration: Pre-load with electrolytes the evening before — HYROX is a sweat-heavy event due to the combination of aerobic intensity and loaded movements. 500ml electrolyte drink with your evening meal helps pre-load sodium for fluid retention.

🍳 Race Morning (3–4 hrs before)

Target: 2–3g/kg carbohydrate. Light to moderate — HYROX events are typically fast-paced enough that a heavy stomach is performance-limiting.

Ideal meal: Porridge with banana and honey. Toast with eggs and a glass of fruit juice. Keep it familiar. The HYROX wall ball station requires a deep squat — a very full stomach makes this significantly more uncomfortable.

60 minutes before: Energy gel or banana. 500ml isotonic drink. This tops up blood glucose without creating a full stomach.

Caffeine: HYROX is one of the events where caffeine has the strongest evidence for benefit. It improves aerobic endurance (the running legs), muscular endurance (the station volume), grip strength, pain tolerance, and power output. 4–6mg/kg, 45 min before your start time. This is non-negotiable for competitive HYROX athletes.

Creatine (if using): 5g with breakfast. The explosive ATP-PCr demand of the sled push, farmers carry, and wall balls is directly supported by creatine loading.

⚡ During the Race

Sub-60 minute competitors (elite/pro): No intra-race nutrition necessary if well fuelled beforehand. Sips of water at stations only. Any solid food or gel at this pace risks GI distress.

60–75 minute competitors: One energy gel at the halfway point (around the sled pull or burpee station) with a mouthful of water. The isotonic nature of gels means taking without water causes GI issues. Time this carefully — you do not want to take a gel going into the wall balls.

75–90+ minute competitors: 1–2 gels during the race (every 30–40 min). Small sips of sports drink between stations where available. The longer the race takes, the more glycogen depletion matters and the more critical intra-race carbs become.

Electrolytes: Sweat rate in HYROX is very high due to the intensity and loaded nature of the stations. Cramping during the farmers carry or sled pull is common in competitors who are dehydrated or electrolyte-depleted. If racing in a warm venue — electrolyte tabs pre-race are strongly recommended.

🔄 Post-Race Recovery

HYROX creates a unique recovery challenge — significant glycogen depletion from the running combined with substantial muscle damage from the loaded stations (especially the sled push, sandbag lunges, and wall balls). Treat it as a hybrid between a half marathon and a hard gym session.

Immediate (0–30 min): 30–40g protein + 60–80g carbs. Chocolate milk or recovery shake. Rehydrate — sweat losses in HYROX can be 1.0–1.5kg per hour at high intensity. Drink 1.5× the weight lost.

Post-race meal (1–2 hrs): High protein (40g+) + high carbohydrate + anti-inflammatory foods. Tart cherry juice strongly recommended — the muscular damage from the station work creates significant DOMS for 24–48 hours post-HYROX, especially in the legs, shoulders, and grip.

If training again within 48–72 hours: Prioritise carbohydrate replenishment at every meal. Keep protein at 2.0–2.2g/kg. The muscular damage from a competitive HYROX event typically requires 48–72 hours for full recovery — don't underestimate it.

Boxing & MMA — Fight Week Nutrition

▼

Why Combat Sport Nutrition Is Different

No other sport combines the nutritional demands of making weight, surviving a water cut, rehydrating under pressure, and then performing at maximum physical and cognitive intensity — sometimes within 24 hours. Get fight week nutrition wrong and a fighter can show up drained, flat, and slower before a punch is thrown. Get it right and they step on the scales drained but step into the cage or ring full, powerful, and sharp. This guide covers the full fight camp arc — from 8 weeks out through to post-fight recovery.

🏕️ Fight Camp Nutrition (8–3 Weeks Out)
Building the body that will fight — performance nutrition during heavy training
▼

Fight camp is the most physically demanding training block in any sport. Two-a-days are common. Sparring, drilling, strength work, and conditioning compound into a daily caloric expenditure that can exceed 4,000–5,000 kcal for a middleweight-class fighter. Underfuelling in camp directly leads to poor sparring quality, slow recovery, increased injury risk, and a fighter arriving at fight week already depleted.

Calorie Target in Camp

Base calculation: Use Mifflin-St Jeor for BMR then apply an activity multiplier of 1.9–2.2 for two-a-day training. Most male fighters need 3,500–5,000 kcal/day during peak camp.

The goal: Maintain or slowly drop body weight during camp through a modest deficit (200–300 kcal below TDEE). Aggressive cuts during camp sacrifice performance. The weight should be managed gradually across 8 weeks — not crashed in the final week.

Weight check: Fighter should arrive at fight week within 5–8% of fight weight having already lost excess body fat progressively. If a fighter is 10%+ over fight weight entering the final week — the weight cut will be dangerous and performance will suffer.

Macros During Camp

Protein: 2.2–2.6g/kg body weight. The highest protein requirement of any sport phase. Camp produces significant muscle damage from sparring collisions, impact absorption, and high training volume. Every gram of protein is justified.

Carbohydrate: 5–7g/kg on training days. Combat sport is glycolytic — rounds are anaerobic, scrambles are explosive, and clinch work is sustained. Glycogen availability directly determines how good the last round of sparring looks versus the first.

Fat: 0.8–1.0g/kg. Adequate for hormonal health, joint lubrication, and fat-soluble vitamin absorption. Don't cut fat aggressively — testosterone and cortisol balance depend on it, particularly under camp stress.

Rest days: Reduce carbs to 3–4g/kg. Keep protein the same. Fat can increase slightly. Total calories drop 400–700 kcal vs training days.

Lean protein sources: Chicken breast, turkey mince, salmon, white fish, eggs, Greek yoghurt, cottage cheese. Rotating sources ensures diverse amino acid and micronutrient profiles.

Complex carbs: White rice (easy to digest), sweet potato, oats, banana, dates. Simple sources pre-workout, complex sources in main meals.

Anti-inflammatory priority: Oily fish 3x/week, colourful vegetables daily, olive oil, berries, ginger and turmeric in cooking. Camp inflammation is systemic — dietary anti-inflammatory load matters.

Avoid during camp: Alcohol (impairs recovery, sleep, reaction time), processed food (hidden sodium disrupts weight management), fast food (inflammatory fat, unpredictable macros). Treat the diet during camp as an extension of the training.

📅 Fight Week — Day by Day (7 Days Out to Fight Night)
The complete countdown — what to eat, when to cut, when to load
▼
⚠️ Pre-condition for this guide: The fighter should be within 5–8% of fight weight entering this week. If more than 8–10% over — this guide cannot safely be compressed into 7 days. The weight cut must begin earlier in camp.

Day 7 — Monday: Normal Training, Normal Eating

Calories: Full TDEE or small deficit (200–300 kcal). This is not yet fight week dietary restriction — the body needs fuel for the final technical sessions.

Macros: Protein 2.2g/kg. Carbs 5g/kg. Fat 0.9g/kg. Normal camp eating.

Training: Light technical session — no hard sparring this week. Drilling, pad work, light BJJ. The body is tapering volume while maintaining sharpness.

Hydration: Begin the water load today — 4 litres across the day. Sip consistently — do not bolt large volumes.

Focus: Sleep. 8–9 hours minimum. The final week's quality depends heavily on the sleep accumulated now.

Day 6 — Tuesday: Water Loading + Sodium Normal

Calories: Moderate deficit — 400–500 kcal below TDEE. Begin pulling slightly from excess body fat and glycogen stores.

Carbs: Drop to 4g/kg. Reducing glycogen slightly begins to reduce the water glycogen binds (1g glycogen = 3–4g water). Muscles may start to look slightly flatter — this is correct and temporary.

Protein: Maintain 2.2–2.4g/kg. Protein intake must remain high to prevent muscle loss during the coming restriction.

Sodium: Keep normal today — don't restrict yet. Sudden sodium restriction triggers compensatory aldosterone release that can cause water retention.

Water: 5 litres. The body's regulatory response to high fluid intake is now fully established — it is excreting water efficiently. This sets up the cut.

Food choices: Begin eliminating obviously processed or high-sodium foods. Cook everything fresh. Weigh and log.

Day 5 — Wednesday: High Water, Low Sodium Begins

Calories: 600–800 kcal below TDEE. Meaningful deficit without performance destruction — there's a light technical session but no demanding training.

Carbs: 3g/kg. Continued glycogen reduction pulling water from the intracellular space.

Sodium: Begin sodium restriction today. Target under 1,000mg/day. Eliminate all processed food, sauces, canned goods, restaurant food. Cook all meals plain.

Water: 6 litres — the highest loading day. The combination of high water + dropping sodium creates the physiological setup for rapid excretion when water is cut tomorrow.

Foods today: Plain chicken breast, plain rice or sweet potato, leafy vegetables (no dressing), eggs, plain oats with water. No flavour additions containing sodium.

Natural diuretics: Asparagus, cucumber, dandelion tea, watermelon. Add these to meals and between meals to support natural water excretion.

Day 4 — Thursday: Taper Water, Continue Low Sodium

Calories: 800–1000 kcal below TDEE. The body is beginning to feel the restriction — energy may be lower. This is manageable with the right mindset and low training volume.

Carbs: 1.5–2g/kg. Glycogen is now meaningfully depleted. Muscles will look smaller and flatter — fighters often panic at this stage and overeat carbs. Don't. The fill-up comes later.

Water: Drop to 2–2.5 litres. The body's aldosterone levels are low from the loading phase — it continues to excrete water at a higher rate than normal even though intake has dropped. This is the payoff from the load.

Sodium: Under 500mg. This is very restrictive — plain food only. Lemon juice or apple cider vinegar can add flavour without sodium.

Protein: 2.0–2.2g/kg — slightly lower than camp due to overall caloric reduction. Still high enough to prevent muscle catabolism.

Sleep: 8–9 hours. Growth hormone released during deep sleep preserves muscle mass during the energy deficit.

Day 3 — Friday: Water Cut Begins

This is the critical day — how aggressive this gets depends on how much weight remains.

Water intake: 1–1.5 litres maximum. Small sips only when thirst is uncomfortable. The body is now excreting more fluid than it takes in.

Sodium: Near zero. Plain foods only. Check labels on everything — most condiments are eliminated.

Calories: 1,000–1,500 kcal below TDEE or simply eat to satiety on protein and vegetables.

Carbs: 0.5–1g/kg. Minimal glycogen intake — the point now is to be as light as possible at weigh-in. Carbs will return after.

If still significantly over weight: Hot bath (15–20 min) or sauna session (15 min) with careful monitoring. Never alone. Electrolyte tablet in small amount of water after each sweat session. Target no more than 1–2 sweating sessions per day.

Training: Light technical session only — drilling, shadowboxing. No cardio that significantly increases sweat rate (depletes further than intended).

Day 2 — Saturday: Final Cut (Weigh-In Day for Some)

Water intake: 500ml–750ml maximum. Sips only when needed. This is the minimum physiological floor — do not go below 500ml.

Food: Light and dry. Small amounts of protein (chicken, egg white). Very low carb. No fruits or vegetables with high water content. The goal is to put as little in as possible while staying functional.

Morning routine: Hot shower to open pores. Light movement — a 20-minute walk or shadowboxing to sweat slightly. Weigh after toilet and before any food or fluid.

If weight is achieved by morning: Maintain restriction until weigh-in. Small sips of water only. Chewing gum can help manage hunger and dry mouth.

Weigh-in: Wear as little as possible. Some fighters urinate immediately before stepping on scales — every gram counts at the final stage.

⚖️ Post-Weigh-In — The Most Critical Window

This is where fight night is won or lost. The rehydration and refuelling protocol between weigh-in and fight is as important as the cut itself.

Immediate (0–30 min post-weigh-in):

Electrolyte drink first — NOT plain water. 500ml of an electrolyte solution containing sodium (1000–1500mg / 1.0–1.5g), potassium (300–500mg), and magnesium. Drinking large volumes of plain water post-cut causes hyponatraemia (sodium dilution) — this has caused serious health events in fighters.

Small amount of fast carbohydrates alongside — banana, white bread with honey, rice cakes, sports drink with sugar. 30–50g carbs to begin glycogen restoration.

30–60 min: Continue electrolyte drinking. Add a small protein source — whey shake or light protein meal. Don't overload the stomach — it has been largely empty and motility is reduced.

1–3 hours post-weigh-in:

Begin the structured refuelling meal. This is the most important meal of fight week.

Target: 60–80g carbohydrate + 40–50g protein + electrolytes + continued fluid intake at 300–400ml every 30 minutes.

Example meal: White rice 250g (cooked) + grilled chicken 200g + sweet potato + electrolyte drink. Simple, digestible, no heavy fats or fibre that slow gastric emptying.

3–12 hours (overnight if AM fight): Continue eating 4–5 meals. Target 3–5g/kg carbohydrate total across the post-weigh-in period. Continue fluid intake to thirst + electrolytes. Total fluid target: 1.5 litres per kg of body weight lost in the cut.

🥊 Fight Day Nutrition
From waking to walking out — what to eat and when
▼

Morning of the Fight

4–5 hours before fight time: The main pre-fight meal. This is the last full meal before competing. Treat it like any pre-match meal — high carbohydrate, moderate protein, low fat and fibre.

Example meal: White rice 200g + chicken breast 150g + banana + electrolyte drink. Avoids heavy fats, raw vegetables, beans, or anything that causes GI discomfort. Familiar food the fighter has eaten many times before.

Total target: 2–3g/kg carbohydrate. 30–40g protein. Under 15g fat.

Coffee/caffeine: 3–5mg/kg 45–60 minutes before fight time. The most evidence-backed fight performance intervention. Do not skip this unless the fighter has caffeine sensitivity or is not habituated to it. New stimulus on fight day = unpredictable response.

60–90 Minutes Before

Light top-up: Banana, energy gel, 200ml sports drink, rice cakes with honey. 20–30g fast carbs. Tops up blood glucose without a heavy stomach.

Hydration: 300–400ml water or light electrolyte drink. Don't go into the fight dehydrated — but don't drink a large volume with a heavy stomach.

Pre-fight supplement protocol:

— Caffeine (3–5mg/kg) 45–60 min before

— Beta-alanine (if using regularly — don't start fight day) contributes to round buffering

— Sodium bicarbonate (0.2–0.3g/kg) 60–90 min before for fights expected to go to the later rounds — provides additional blood buffering. Must have been practised in training to avoid GI distress.

Between Rounds — Corner Nutrition

The corner is a nutrition window. Most fighters spit out their water — but swallowing small amounts of a carbohydrate-containing drink has measurable performance benefits in extended fights.

Between every round: 50–100ml of isotonic sports drink (with carbohydrate — not plain water). Even 30g of carbohydrate consumed across a fight has measurable effects on late-round performance in bouts of 3+ rounds. Some fighters use energy gels between rounds 2–3 of a 5-round championship fight.

Half-time for 3-minute rounds: The 60 seconds between rounds is limited. Sip, don't gulp. Gulping causes GI discomfort. Teach the fighter to take a mouthful, keep it briefly, then swallow or spit depending on stomach state.

Ice: Ice pack on back of neck and wrists between rounds reduces core temperature and delays fatigue. Physiologically significant in warm venues or long fights.

Smelling salts: Common in boxing — ammonia inhalant stimulates the sympathetic nervous system. Sharpen arousal and focus between rounds. Not for regular training use — reserve for competition only.

⚖️ Weight Category Strategy
How much to cut, when to move up, and managing long-term weight class decisions
▼

How Much Should a Fighter Cut?

The performance-safety sweet spot: A cut of 3–8% of body weight (with 24+ hours recovery) allows near-complete rehydration and performance recovery. A 70kg fighter cutting 3–5kg is manageable. A 70kg fighter cutting 10kg+ is dangerous and the performance cost is severe — even with a 24-hour recovery window.

The diminishing returns: Each additional kg cut past 5–6% provides less competitive advantage (bigger, but more drained at fight time) and more risk. Research consistently shows cognitive function — reaction time, decision-making speed — is not fully restored within 24 hours after cuts exceeding 7–8%.

The calculation: Natural walking weight minus fight weight = cut. If a fighter walks at 75kg and fights at 70kg — a 5kg/7% cut is manageable with 24hr recovery. If they walk at 80kg — consider moving up a weight class.

When to Move Up a Weight Class

Signs a fighter should move up:

— Walking weight is consistently 10%+ above fight weight and the cut is becoming increasingly difficult

— Repeated cuts are affecting recovery, mood, and training quality during camp

— The fighter is losing muscle mass to make weight — arriving at the fight structurally smaller than opponents

— Health markers deteriorating — kidney function, hormonal panels, immune function

— Post-weigh-in rehydration is not restoring performance within 24 hours

The strategic argument: A well-fuelled 70kg fighter competing in the 75kg class is often more competitive than the same fighter making a brutal cut to 70kg. Being the bigger, fresher fighter on the night frequently outweighs the size advantage of being in a lower class drained.

🥊 vs 🤼 Boxing vs MMA — Key Nutritional Differences
Same principles, different demands — how nutrition strategy changes between the two sports
▼

Boxing-Specific Considerations

Higher aerobic demand: Boxing rounds are predominantly aerobic with explosive anaerobic bursts. A 12-round championship fight lasts 36 minutes of action. Aerobic base nutrition (carbohydrate availability, VO₂ max support via beetroot/nitrates) is particularly important.

Longer rounds in some formats: 3-minute rounds in professional boxing are longer than many MMA rounds. Between-round nutrition (isotonic drink sips) provides proportionally greater benefit over a 10–12 round fight.

Head impact nutrition: Boxing involves higher cumulative head impact than most sports. Omega-3 (EPA/DHA at 3–4g/day) has emerging evidence for reducing neurological inflammation from repeated sub-concussive impacts. Creatine also shows neuroprotective properties in head impact sports. Both should be non-negotiables in professional boxers.

Chin and jaw: Body composition matters aesthetically less in boxing — but structural strength of the neck and jaw matters. Nutrition supporting muscle and tendon health (protein, collagen) around the neck and jaw is relevant.

MMA-Specific Considerations

Higher overall physical demand: MMA uses striking, wrestling, clinch, and ground work simultaneously. Caloric expenditure per training hour is higher than boxing. Nutrition must fuel a broader range of physical qualities.

More diverse energy system demands: A takedown scramble is explosive PCr-dependent. A wrestling round is aerobic-lactic. A submission attempt is near-maximal. The creatine and beta-alanine protocol is more clearly justified in MMA than in boxing.

Weight cutting culture: MMA has historically had more aggressive cutting culture than boxing. Coaches must be aware of and push back against extreme cuts — the sport has had a number of deaths and near-deaths from water cutting. Ethical responsibility to the athlete.

Grappling nutrition: Grip strength and forearm endurance are limiting factors in grappling. Creatine (PCr for grip), collagen (connective tissue in fingers and wrists), and iron (oxygen to forearm muscles) are specific to this demand.

Ground and pound recovery: Taking shots from the top or bottom position while grappling is a different neurological stress to standing boxing. Recovery from high-impact ground work benefits from the same anti-inflammatory protocol as combat sport generally — omega-3, tart cherry, curcumin.

🔄 Post-Fight Recovery Nutrition
Repairing damage, restoring hormones, and rebuilding for the next camp
▼

A fight creates a level of physiological stress that exceeds almost any training session. Oxidative stress from exertion, tissue damage from strikes and grappling, neurological stress from head impact, and hormonal disruption from the weight cut all compound. Proper post-fight nutrition is critical for full recovery and long-term health.

Night of the Fight (0–4 Hours)

Hydration first: Continue electrolyte drinking. The cut and the fight together create substantial fluid debt — rehydrate across the evening, not in one sitting.

Post-fight meal: This is not a strict performance nutrition meal — it's a recovery and celebration meal. A proper mixed meal covering protein, carbs, and fat is appropriate. The fighter has earned it. Avoid extreme excess that causes GI distress.

Alcohol consideration: Post-fight alcohol is culturally common in combat sport. Be pragmatic — after a fight is not the context for strict nutritional intervention. However, if the fighter is competing again within 4–6 weeks, minimising alcohol the first few days post-fight accelerates the recovery process (alcohol impairs muscle protein synthesis, sleep quality, and inflammation resolution).

Days 1–7 Post-Fight

Anti-inflammatory priority: The body needs to clear oxidative byproducts and reduce inflammation. Omega-3 (4g/day), tart cherry (30ml x2), turmeric with piperine, and colourful vegetables support this. Avoid excessive processed food and alcohol which amplify inflammation.

Protein — muscle repair: 2.0–2.2g/kg/day. Striking impact creates muscle damage beyond what training produces. Full protein intake supports repair. Collagen + vitamin C (15g + 1g) daily for connective tissue repair — particularly if cuts, bruises, or joint strain were sustained.

Sleep: The most important recovery tool post-fight. Cortisol is acutely elevated post-competition. Magnesium glycinate, L-theanine, and tart cherry all support sleep quality during this window.

Return to training: Minimum 1 week off after a fight. Minimum 2–3 weeks before resuming hard sparring. The nervous system, hormonal system, and structural tissue all need time to recover fully. Rushing back increases injury risk and undermines performance in the next camp.

💊 Fight Day Supplement Stack
Exact doses, exact timing — only use what has been practised in camp
▼

2 Hours Pre-Fight

Caffeine: 3–5mg/kg body weight. For a 70kg fighter: 210–350mg. Anhydrous caffeine tablet is more reliable than coffee (consistent dose, faster absorption). Improves power output 3–7%, reaction time, decision-making, and pain tolerance. Non-negotiable.

Citrulline Malate: 6–8g. Nitric oxide precursor. Increases blood flow, reduces the oxygen cost of effort, and improves muscular endurance across rounds. Take 45–60 min before fighting.

Creatine: 5g (if taken throughout camp — just maintain daily dose). The PCr reservoir built over weeks of supplementation is what provides the explosive ATP for power punches and takedown drives.

Sodium Bicarbonate (advanced protocol): 0.2–0.3g/kg body weight, 60–90 min pre-fight. Increases blood bicarbonate, enhancing the buffering of hydrogen ions produced during intense rounds. Proven to improve performance in efforts lasting 1–7 minutes. Must be practised in training first — GI distress in 40–50% of users. Take with a meal to reduce this.

30 Minutes Pre-Fight

Beta-Alanine: Only if already taking throughout camp (3.2g/day). The muscle carnosine built over weeks is what buffers lactic acid — a single pre-fight dose does nothing. This is a camp supplement, not a fight day supplement. Take only if part of the routine.

Between rounds (corner nutrition): 50–100ml of a carbohydrate-containing isotonic drink. Swallow — don't spit. Even 30g of carbohydrate consumed across a fight has measurable effects on round 4–5 performance. If the fighter cannot tolerate a drink between rounds — mouth rinsing with a carbohydrate solution has a neural effect (brain detects carbohydrate and improves output) even when spat out.

Ice between rounds: Ice pack on back of neck and wrists reduces core temperature and delays fatigue meaningfully in warm venues. Instruct the corner to have this ready in every session and fight.

Avoid on fight day: New supplements or foods not previously trialled. High-fibre foods (GI distress during fighting). Alcohol. Excessive caffeine beyond recommended dose (anxiety, jitteriness). Large volumes of plain water post-weigh-in (hyponatraemia risk).

🥊 vs 🤼 Boxing vs MMA — Same Day Differences
Where the two sports require different approaches on fight day
▼

Boxing Fight Day Specifics

Round length: 3 minutes (professional). Shorter explosive rounds mean higher anaerobic demand per round. Carbohydrate availability and the PCr system are the primary energy sources.

Number of rounds: 4–12 rounds. A 12-round fight is 36 minutes of boxing — significant aerobic component in later rounds. Intra-fight carbohydrate becomes more important in longer fights.

Pre-fight meal timing: Stick to 4–5 hours before. Boxers have been known to eat too close to the fight causing nausea — the standing, rotating, and head movement after a large meal is particularly problematic.

Head impact recovery: Post-fight anti-inflammatory protocol is critical. Omega-3 (3–4g EPA/DHA) daily throughout camp and especially post-fight for neurological protection.

MMA Fight Day Specifics

Round length: 5 minutes (professional MMA). The extra 2 minutes vs boxing significantly increases the aerobic contribution and the importance of between-round recovery nutrition.

Multi-dimensional demand: The grappling, wrestling, and ground work of MMA increase the muscular fatigue profile beyond pure striking. Creatine becomes even more important — PCr system fuels the explosive scrambles and takedown attempts.

Weight cut urgency: MMA weight cuts are often more aggressive than boxing. The rehydration timeline (above) is therefore more critical — do not shortcut the electrolyte protocol. A fighter who arrives dehydrated will gas out in round 2 regardless of fitness.

Grip and forearm: Grappling creates forearm and grip fatigue not present in boxing. Creatine (PCr in forearms) and adequate hydration both directly affect grappling output in later rounds.

Water Loading, Water Cutting & Competition Prep

▼
⚠️ Professional Reference: Water manipulation is an advanced protocol used for physique competitions, photoshoots, and combat sport weight cuts. Done incorrectly it is dangerous. Always supervise clients through this process. Electrolyte monitoring is essential. Never recommend aggressive water cutting to untrained clients or those with cardiovascular or kidney conditions.

Why Manipulate Water?

The human body is approximately 60% water. Subcutaneous water (under the skin) blurs muscle definition — even a lean physique can look flat and undefined if holding excess water. For a photoshoot, bodybuilding stage, or weighing in for combat sport, manipulating water in and out of the body can dramatically change appearance or weight within 48–72 hours. The process is temporary — the body re-equilibrates within 24–48 hours after normal eating and drinking resume.

📈 Phase 1 — Water Loading (Days 1–4 before event)
Flood the system to trigger the response that makes the cut effective
▼

Why load water before cutting it? When you suddenly stop drinking water after a period of high intake, the body is still producing aldosterone and ADH (antidiuretic hormone) at low levels — suppressed by the high fluid intake. This means the body excretes water more readily in the first 12–24 hours after stopping. Without a load phase, cutting water simply triggers the thirst and retention mechanisms immediately. Load first = cut more effectively.

Water Loading Protocol

Day 5 (5 days out): 4 litres of water

Day 4 (4 days out): 5 litres of water

Day 3 (3 days out): 6 litres of water

Day 2 (2 days out): 6 litres of water — final high day

Spread intake evenly across waking hours. Don't consume large volumes at once — hyponatraemia risk.

Sodium During Loading Phase

Keep sodium normal to moderate during the loading phase. Extreme sodium restriction during loading can cause hyponatraemia — dangerously low blood sodium from excess water without electrolytes.

Some coaches use moderate sodium loading alongside water loading (days 5–3) then drop sodium sharply on days 2–1. The sodium drop with maintained high water intake triggers the excretion response.

Foods to avoid: Processed food, tinned food, soy sauce, restaurant meals — hidden sodium makes control impossible.

Foods to use: Fresh lean protein, vegetables, plain rice or sweet potato, egg whites — clean, controllable macros.

📉 Phase 2 — Water Cutting (48 Hours Out)
Eliminate subcutaneous water to enhance muscle definition and separation
▼

48–24 Hours Out — Taper

Day 1 (24 hours out): Reduce to 1–1.5 litres total. Sip throughout — no large volumes at once.

Continue low sodium: Sodium draws water into the bloodstream and under the skin. Eliminate all processed food. Use herbs and spices for flavour.

Carbohydrate: Moderate to low. Glycogen stores water — 1g glycogen binds ~3g water. Some coaches use a strategic carb depletion here for maximum subcutaneous water removal.

Diuretic foods (natural): Asparagus, cucumber, leafy greens, dandelion tea, lemon water. These provide mild diuretic effect without pharmaceutical risk.

24–12 Hours Out — Final Cut

Water intake: 500ml–1 litre maximum. Take small sips only when necessary. This is the critical window.

Sodium: Virtually zero. Plain chicken, plain rice only. Weigh food.

Caffeine: Mild diuretic effect — black coffee (no milk, no sweetener) twice daily. The caffeine also stimulates GI motility — helpful for clearing gut weight.

Hot baths / sauna (optional, caution): 15–20 minutes in hot bath or sauna can pull 500g–1kg of water through sweat. Must rehydrate electrolytes carefully afterwards. Risk of cardiovascular stress — monitor closely.

Posing / pumping: Blood flow to muscles draws water intramuscularly (where it helps definition) and away from subcutaneous space.

Competition Morning / Photoshoot Day

Upon waking: No food or water for the first hour after waking. Allow morning cortisol peak to mobilise any remaining water.

30–60 minutes before stage/shoot: Begin carb loading (see below). A small sip of water with the carbs is acceptable.

Pump-up: 15–20 minutes of resistance work before going on stage or in front of the camera. Blood is pumped into muscle — increasing apparent muscle fullness and vascularity. Target the body parts being photographed.

Topical oil/tan: Posing oil and stage tan both enhance definition under lights. Apply evenly — patches of lighter skin flatten appearance.

🍠 Carbohydrate Loading (Peak Week)
Fill the muscle with glycogen to create fullness and the pumped 3D look
▼

Why carb load? Fully depleted glycogen stores make muscles look flat, small, and undefined — even at very low body fat. Glycogen binds water intramuscularly, creating the round, full, 3D appearance associated with stage-ready physiques. The goal is glycogen-full muscles with no subcutaneous water on top.

Carb Depletion Phase (Days 5–3)

Some competitors use a depletion phase to maximise glycogen supercompensation on load day. 3 days of very low carbohydrate (under 50g/day) empties glycogen stores. Muscles temporarily look flat — this is the depletion working.

When carbs are reintroduced, the depleted muscles over-compensate and fill beyond their starting glycogen level (supercompensation). Not all coaches use depletion — individual response varies significantly.

Carb Loading Phase (Days 2–1)

Day 2 (2 days out): 3–5g/kg body weight of carbohydrate. Complex sources — white rice, sweet potato, oats. Watch sodium content of all foods.

Day 1 (morning of event): 1–2g/kg moderate-to-fast carbs 2–3 hours before. On stage or on shoot: 30–50g fast carbs 30 minutes before pump-up (rice cakes, banana, gummy sweets, grape juice). Some competitors use a single can of beer or glass of wine — the carbohydrates fill muscles while the mild diuretic effect of alcohol assists with final water clearance. Use with caution.

🥊 Combat Sport Water Cuts (Weight Classes)
Rapid weight reduction for weigh-ins — highest risk protocol
▼
⚠️ EXTREME CAUTION: Combat sport water cuts carry genuine health risks including renal failure, cardiac arrhythmia, and death. Several athletes have died from aggressive weight cutting. This information is provided for harm reduction. Always advocate for the smallest possible cut and adequate recovery time between weigh-in and competition.

Safe Parameters

Maximum recommended cut: 5–8% of body weight. A 70kg athlete cutting more than 5.6kg of water is entering danger territory.

Minimum recovery time: 2 hours between weigh-in and competition is dangerously insufficient. 24 hours is the minimum for meaningful rehydration and performance recovery. Advocate for same-day weigh-ins where possible.

Method hierarchy (safest to riskiest):

1. Diet — manipulate water-holding macros (glycogen, sodium) in the days before.

2. Sweat — controlled exercise in warm conditions or sauna (15–20 min maximum sessions with monitoring).

3. Restriction — reducing water intake (no less than 500ml/day).

NEVER recommend: Diuretic medications, plastic suits for extended sessions, excessive sauna time, or cuts exceeding 10% body weight.

Rehydration Protocol (Post-Weigh-In)

Immediate: Electrolyte drink — not plain water. Sodium (1500–2000mg), potassium, and magnesium alongside 500–750ml of fluid. Drinking large volumes of plain water after a dehydration cut causes electrolyte dilution (hyponatraemia) — more dangerous than dehydration.

Hour 1–2: Continue electrolyte drinks at 500ml every 30–45 minutes. Add carbohydrates — 1g/kg of fast carbs to begin glycogen restoration.

Hours 2–12: Full recovery meal. High carb, moderate protein, low fat. Continue drinking electrolyte fluid to thirst. Target 1.5× the weight lost in fluid over 4–6 hours total.

Sleep: If overnight recovery is possible — this is the best rehydration window. The body restores intracellular fluid more effectively during sleep.

Performance recovery: Strength and power return within 24 hours of proper rehydration. Cognitive function (reaction time, decision-making) returns within 2–4 hours of moderate rehydration — critical for technical performance in combat sport.

Carbohydrate Depletion & Loading — Full Protocol

▼

Why Deplete Before You Load?

Carbohydrate depletion followed by loading triggers glycogen supercompensation — the muscles store 15–20% more glycogen than their normal baseline. The result: fuller, rounder muscles on competition or photoshoot day. The strategic timing of depletion and loading is the difference between looking flat on stage and looking the best you have ever looked.

📉 Carbohydrate Depletion Phase (Days 5–3 Out)
Empty the tank so you can overfill it — the mechanism behind supercompensation
▼

The physiology: When glycogen stores are depleted, the enzymes responsible for glycogen synthesis (glycogen synthase) are upregulated — they become hypersensitive. When carbohydrates are reintroduced, these hypersensitive enzymes drive carbohydrate into muscle at a higher rate than normal, resulting in glycogen storage 20–40% above pre-depletion levels. Full muscles. Fuller look.

Depletion Protocol

Day 5 (5 days out) — Begin depletion:
Carbs: Under 100g (1g/kg for a 70–80kg athlete). Keep protein high (2.4g/kg). Fat moderate. Calories in mild deficit. Continue light training — exercise accelerates glycogen depletion.

Day 4 (4 days out) — Deep depletion:
Carbs: Under 50g total. Mostly from vegetables. Protein: 2.4–2.6g/kg. Fat: 1.0–1.2g/kg. Muscles will look visibly smaller and flatter — this is the depletion working, not a problem. Resist the urge to eat carbs early.

Day 3 (3 days out) — Final depletion day:
Carbs: Under 50g (vegetable sources only). Same protein. Include a brief high-rep training session (15–20 reps per set, lighter weight) — this is glycogen-depleting exercise that maximises the supercompensation response without the muscle damage of heavy loading.

What to Eat During Depletion

Protein sources (high throughout):
Chicken breast, turkey mince, white fish, egg whites, lean beef, Greek yoghurt (plain). These maintain muscle tissue during the low-calorie depletion phase.

Fat sources (moderate):
Olive oil, avocado, eggs (whole), small amounts of nuts. Fat provides satiety and hormonal stability during carb restriction.

Carb sources allowed (<50g total):
Leafy greens (spinach, kale, rocket), cucumber, courgette, broccoli, peppers. These provide micronutrients and fibre with minimal carbohydrate impact.

What to avoid:
Bread, rice, pasta, potato, fruit, oats, dairy with lactose, sports drinks. Any starchy or sugary food during this phase undermines the depletion.

Symptoms you will experience:
Brain fog, low energy, flat muscles, irritability, reduced exercise performance. All normal. All temporary. All indicators the protocol is working.

📈 Carbohydrate Loading Phase (Days 2–0)
Fill the supercompensated muscle with glycogen — the fullness comes here
▼

Day 2 (2 days out) — Begin Loading

Carbs: 4–6g/kg body weight. For a 75kg athlete: 300–450g carbs. This is significantly more than typical daily intake — it is deliberately high to trigger supercompensation.

Sources: White rice, sweet potato, oats, white bread, banana, pasta, rice cakes. Simple to moderate GI — not pure sugar, not high fibre. The muscles need to store it, not just spike blood sugar.

Protein: Reduce to 1.8g/kg — lower than camp levels. High protein competes for digestive resources and can cause bloating that undermines the full look.

Fat: Keep low (under 0.6g/kg) — fat slows gastric emptying and increases water retention subcutaneously. Low fat on loading day means better definition.

Sodium: Begin mild restriction today (<1,500mg/1.5g). Sodium holds subcutaneous water — the goal is glycogen-bound intramuscular water, not sodium-held subcutaneous water.

Training: Light session only (20–30 min technical or light lifting). Movement aids glycogen uptake but heavy training delays the fill-up.

Day 1 (1 day out / Day Before) — Peak Loading

Carbs: 5–8g/kg body weight. The highest carb day. 375–600g for a 75kg athlete. Spread across 5–6 meals. The muscles are maximally receptive today — don't under-eat here.

Meal structure:
Breakfast: Oats + banana + honey
Mid-morning: Rice cakes + jam
Lunch: White rice + chicken
Afternoon: Sweet potato + tuna
Dinner: White pasta + lean meat sauce
Pre-bed: Casein shake + rice cakes

Water: Normal to slightly reduced (1.5–2.5 litres). Not cutting water today — maintaining hydration while avoiding subcutaneous accumulation.

Sodium: Under 500mg. Near-zero processed food. All meals cooked plain.

Signs it is working: Muscles will visibly fill back out and look fuller than during depletion. Vascularity may increase as glycogen is stored intramuscularly. The mirror will show the change if the protocol has been executed correctly.

Day 0 — Event Day Nutrition

Morning (if early event): 30–50g fast carbs on waking. Rice cakes, banana, honey, or a small amount of jelly sweets. Purpose: top up liver glycogen depleted during overnight fast without adding volume to the stomach.

30–60 min before stage/shoot: 30–50g fast carbs: banana, gummy bears, grape juice, white bread with jam. Spikes blood sugar for the insulin and glycogen drive that creates the final pump.

Pre-pump protocol: 15–20 minutes of resistance work targeting the body parts being photographed or judged. Blood is driven into muscle — the pump is a real, physiological event that increases the appearance of muscle size and separation.

Caffeine: 200–300mg caffeine 30–45 minutes before. Vasodilator and CNS stimulant. Increases blood flow and enhances the pump quality. Also improves mental sharpness and posing energy.

Citrulline malate: 6–8g 45 min before. Nitric oxide precursor — enhances vasodilation and pump quality further.

Creatine monohydrate: If loading was maintained in prep — continue 5g/day. The intramuscular water from creatine contributes to the full, round look.

Avoid: Anything that causes GI distress (dairy, high fibre, beans, new foods). The stage or camera is not the place to discover a food sensitivity. Stick entirely to foods you have eaten many times before.

Fasting — Protocols, Benefits & Risks

▼

What Is Fasting?

Fasting is a deliberate period of abstaining from caloric intake. It ranges from simple 12-hour overnight fasts to extended multi-day protocols. The evidence base for fasting is growing but nuanced — benefits are real in certain populations, but fasting is not universally superior to standard caloric restriction and carries specific risks that coaches must understand.

⏱️ Intermittent Fasting (IF) Protocols
16:8, 5:2, OMAD, and other time-restricted eating approaches
▼

Common IF Protocols

16:8 (Leangains) — Most Popular: 16-hour fast, 8-hour eating window. Typically skip breakfast, eat 12pm–8pm. Sustainable for most people. Can be adapted around training (eat post-workout within the window).

18:6: 18-hour fast, 6-hour window. More restrictive. Effective for fat loss but harder to hit protein targets in a shorter window.

20:4 (Warrior Diet): One large evening meal plus light snacking during the day. Most difficult to maintain adequate protein intake.

5:2: 5 days of normal eating, 2 non-consecutive days of 500–600 kcal. Good for people who struggle with daily restriction. Less protein concern than daily IF.

OMAD (One Meal a Day): Extreme. Very difficult to hit protein targets. Not recommended for athletes or those prioritising muscle mass.

Alternate Day Fasting (ADF): Fast every other day. Effective but compliance is poor long-term. Significant muscle loss risk without adequate protein on eating days.

What Actually Happens During a Fast

0–12 hours: Normal fed state transitions to early fasting. Insulin declines. Glycogen begins to be mobilised for energy. No meaningful metabolic changes yet.

12–18 hours: Liver glycogen partly depleted. Fat oxidation increases. Growth hormone secretion increases (protecting muscle). Blood glucose stable. Mild ketone production beginning. This is the window most IF protocols target.

18–24 hours: Autophagy (cellular cleanup) significantly upregulated. Ketone production increases. Fat is the primary fuel source. Insulin sensitivity improved. This is where many of the proposed fasting benefits occur.

24–48 hours (extended fast): Significant autophagy. Stem cell activation. Immune system reset (some evidence). Muscle protein synthesis is suppressed — muscle loss risk increases significantly, especially without training stimulus.

48–72 hours: Significant metabolic adaptations. Ketosis. Benefits for neurological health (BDNF increase). Meaningful muscle loss risk without adequate protein cycling.

✅ Benefits of Fasting — What the Evidence Shows
Evidence-rated benefits across metabolic, hormonal, and cognitive domains
▼

Well-Evidenced Benefits

Fat loss (Grade A): IF produces comparable fat loss to continuous caloric restriction when total calories are matched. The advantage is compliance — many people find restricting eating hours easier than counting calories continuously. The metabolic advantage of IF is modest beyond the caloric restriction it creates.

Insulin sensitivity (Grade A): Multiple RCTs confirm improved fasting insulin and HOMA-IR after IF protocols. Particularly impactful in those with insulin resistance, pre-diabetes, or metabolic syndrome. Fasting lowers insulin, which reduces fat storage and increases fat mobilisation.

Blood pressure (Grade B): Several trials show significant reductions in systolic and diastolic BP after IF protocols, independent of weight loss.

Triglycerides and LDL (Grade B): Reductions in triglycerides and LDL cholesterol seen in multiple IF trials. HDL changes are more variable.

Growth hormone (Grade B): GH rises significantly during fasting — a muscle-protective mechanism. 24-hour fasting has been shown to increase GH secretion 5-fold in some studies. This partly explains why IF doesn't cause as much muscle loss as continuous caloric restriction.

Promising But Emerging Evidence

Autophagy (Grade B): Fasting upregulates autophagy — cellular cleanup that removes damaged proteins, organelles, and misfolded proteins. Strongly implicated in longevity, cancer prevention, and neurodegenerative disease prevention. Most evidence in animal models — human autophagy measurement is technically difficult.

Cognitive function (Grade B): Short-term fasting increases BDNF (brain-derived neurotrophic factor) and may improve focus, mental clarity, and learning. Many people report subjective cognitive enhancement during fasting. Ketones provide an efficient alternative fuel for the brain.

Inflammation (Grade B): CRP and other inflammatory markers are reduced by IF protocols. Partly due to weight loss, partly due to independent metabolic effects.

Longevity / healthspan (Grade C in humans): Animal evidence is strong. Caloric restriction extends lifespan in every animal model studied. Human longevity data for IF specifically is not yet available — population studies on caloric restriction are promising but not definitive.

Gut microbiome (Grade C): Emerging evidence that fasting windows allow the gut microbiome to reset and improve diversity. Early data only.

⚠️ Risks of Fasting — Who Should Be Cautious
Muscle loss, hormonal disruption, training performance, and medical contraindications
▼

Risks for Athletes

Muscle protein breakdown: During a fast, the body uses some muscle protein for gluconeogenesis (making glucose). Adequate protein intake within the eating window (2.0–2.4g/kg) and resistance training significantly blunt this — but very extended fasts or OMAD protocols make hitting protein targets extremely difficult.

Training performance: Fasted training reduces high-intensity performance (glycogen-dependent). Strength, power, and sprint performance are all impaired in a fasted state. Zone 2 aerobic training is well-tolerated fasted. Never perform heavy compound lifting, speed work, or competition prep training in a fully fasted state.

Recovery: Post-workout muscle protein synthesis is maximally stimulated when protein is available. If training falls at the end of a fasting window with no protein intake for hours afterwards — recovery is significantly compromised.

Hormonal disruption in women: Women are more sensitive to caloric restriction and fasting than men. Prolonged fasting can disrupt the hypothalamic-pituitary-ovarian axis, disrupting menstrual cycles and hormonal balance. Shorter fasting windows (14:10) are generally better tolerated than 16:8+ in women, particularly athletic women with high energy demands.

Medical Contraindications

Type 1 Diabetes: Without insulin, fasting causes diabetic ketoacidosis. Absolute contraindication without very close medical supervision.

Type 2 Diabetes on insulin or sulfonylureas: Hypoglycaemia risk. Significant medical supervision and medication adjustment required.

History of eating disorders: Fasting protocols can trigger or exacerbate restriction behaviours in clients with anorexia, orthorexia, or other disordered eating histories. Strong contraindication — discuss with their mental health support team first.

Pregnancy and breastfeeding: Contraindicated. Fetal development requires continuous nutrient delivery.

Underweight (BMI <18.5): Insufficient energy reserves. Fasting risks further muscle and fat loss in those without reserves.

Children and adolescents: Growth requires consistent nutrient availability. Not appropriate.

Medications requiring food: Many medications must be taken with food. Always check with a pharmacist.

Kidney disease: Protein metabolism during fasting may be contraindicated. GP review required.

The Honest Verdict on Fasting for Body Composition

IF works — but not for the reasons usually marketed. It works primarily because time-restricted eating makes caloric restriction easier to maintain. The metabolic advantages (autophagy, GH, insulin sensitivity) are real but represent a modest additional benefit, not a transformation of body composition rules.

For fat loss: IF is as effective as continuous restriction when calories are equal. The choice comes down to adherence. If a client finds skipping breakfast easy and not thinking about food until midday reduces their total intake — IF is an excellent tool. If they're miserable and obsessing over when they can eat — it's not the right tool for them.

For muscle building: IF is not optimal. Muscle protein synthesis is stimulated by protein meals spread across the day (every 3–5 hours). Compressing all eating into 6–8 hours means fewer MPS stimulation events per day. Serious muscle builders should prioritise protein distribution over fasting windows.

For general health: A daily eating window of 10–12 hours (the equivalent of not eating for 2 hours after waking and stopping 2 hours before sleep) captures most of the health benefits with minimal risk. This is a sustainable and evidence-backed approach for most clients.

🏋️ Fasting + Training — How to Combine Them
Practical protocols for athletes who want IF benefits without performance sacrifice
▼

Early Trainers (6–9am)

Option 1 — Fed training: Break the fast with protein + carbs before training (6am). Train at 8am. Open the eating window for the full day. Best for performance.

Option 2 — Fasted training + immediate break: Train fasted. Immediately post-workout — whey shake + banana. This breaks the fast at the optimal time (post-workout MPS window). Then eat normally until 8pm. 10–12 hour fast, not 16.

Option 3 — BCAAs pre-fasted training: 10g BCAAs before fasted training blunts muscle protein breakdown without meaningfully breaking the fast (no insulin response). Then break fast post-workout. Suitable for those prioritising both fasting and training quality.

Evening Trainers (5–8pm)

Best setup for IF + evening training: Skip breakfast, eat 12pm–8pm (16:8). Train at 5–7pm within the eating window. Have post-workout meal at 7–8pm then close the window. This is the most performance-friendly IF setup — training is never fasted, recovery nutrition is available immediately.

Recovery nutrition within IF: The post-workout meal (within the eating window) should be: 30–40g protein + 60–80g carbohydrate. This maximises MPS and glycogen replenishment within the time-restricted window.

Pre-workout within IF: If training at 5pm and the eating window opened at 12pm — the athlete has had 5 hours of nutrition before training. Performance should be unaffected. Have a carb-containing meal 2–3 hours before training within the window.

Goal-Specific Nutrition Plans

Fat Loss Nutrition
TDEE − 300–500 kcal
▼

Priority 1 — Protein: Set at 2.0–2.4g/kg body weight. This is higher than for muscle gain specifically to protect lean mass during the caloric deficit. The body is in a catabolic state during a deficit — adequate protein is the primary muscle-preservation tool alongside resistance training.

Priority 2 — Fibre: 30–40g per day minimum. High-fibre foods (vegetables, legumes, wholegrains) are voluminous and filling despite relatively low caloric density. This is the most practical strategy for managing hunger on a deficit.

Carb distribution: Front-load carbohydrates around training. Reduce carbs in the evening meals if not training in the evening. This is not magic — it's a practical way to ensure carbohydrates fuel performance rather than being stored.

Foods that support fat loss: High-volume, low-calorie foods — all vegetables, berries, lean proteins, Greek yoghurt, cottage cheese, eggs, oats. These fill the stomach without filling the calorie budget.

Caloric density awareness: Replace high-calorie-density foods with lower-density alternatives. Swap olive oil drizzled freely → measured 1 tsp. Swap peanut butter × 2 tbsp → 1 tbsp. Swap full-fat dairy → lower-fat versions. These small swaps add up to 200–400 kcal/day without reducing meal volume.

Sample fat loss day (80kg male, 2000 kcal, 175g protein):

Breakfast: 5 eggs scrambled + 50g oats + 200g berries (480 kcal, 40g protein)

Lunch: 200g chicken breast + 150g rice (cooked) + large salad with 1 tsp olive oil (560 kcal, 50g protein)

Snack: 200g Greek yoghurt 0% + 30g whey protein mixed in (250 kcal, 45g protein)

Dinner: 200g salmon + 300g roasted vegetables + 150g sweet potato (520 kcal, 45g protein)

Total: ~1810 kcal, ~180g protein. Room for a snack or flexible with tracking.

Muscle Gain Nutrition
TDEE + 200–400 kcal
▼

Caloric surplus: A modest surplus (200–400 kcal) is sufficient for muscle gain while minimising fat accumulation. The body can only build approximately 0.2–0.5 kg of muscle per week (natural, trained male) — calories beyond that are stored as fat. Eating more does not accelerate muscle gain above this ceiling; it only increases fat gain.

Protein: Set at 1.6–2.2g/kg. Muscle gain does not require as high a protein intake as a cutting phase — in a caloric surplus, the anti-catabolic effect of adequate calories reduces the protein requirement somewhat. However, 2g/kg is a practical and safe target for most.

Carbohydrates are your friend: Adequate carbohydrate intake supports training performance — the foundation of muscle gain. Higher carbohydrate intake during a surplus is appropriate. Depleted glycogen leads to reduced training quality, which limits the primary stimulus for muscle growth. Aim for 4–6g/kg body weight.

Don't neglect fats: Fat is essential for testosterone production. Males eating insufficient dietary fat (<0.5g/kg) have measurably lower testosterone levels. Keep fat at minimum 0.8–1g/kg even during high-carbohydrate bulking phases.

Sample lean bulk day (80kg male, 2800 kcal, 160g protein):

Breakfast: 4 eggs + 100g oats + whole milk + banana (720 kcal, 40g protein)

Snack: Protein shake + 30g oats + 1 tbsp peanut butter (450 kcal, 35g protein)

Lunch: 250g lean beef + 200g rice (cooked) + vegetables (680 kcal, 55g protein)

Post-workout: Protein shake + banana (250 kcal, 25g protein)

Dinner: 250g chicken + 200g pasta + tomato sauce + vegetables (700 kcal, 50g protein)

Total: ~2800 kcal, ~205g protein.

Body Recomposition Nutrition
~TDEE (maintenance calories)
▼

Body recomposition — simultaneously losing fat and gaining muscle — is possible at maintenance calories for beginners, returning trainees, and those with higher body fat percentages. The body uses stored body fat to fuel the caloric requirements of muscle building.

Key requirements for recomposition: Progressive resistance training, protein at 2–2.4g/kg, calories at maintenance (or very slight deficit of 100–200 kcal), consistent sleep (7–9 hours — GH is secreted primarily during sleep), and patience. Recomposition is slower than dedicated bulking or cutting but produces excellent results without the extremes of either.

Best suited for: Beginners (first 6–12 months of training), returning trainees (muscle memory effect), overweight individuals (body fat provides energy for muscle building), and those who simply don't want to bulk/cut cyclically.

Not ideal for: Advanced trainees who are already lean (<12% body fat male, <20% female) — dedicated bulking and cutting phases produce faster results at that stage.

Athletic Performance Nutrition
Sport-specific — high carb priority
▼

Carbohydrate periodisation: Match carbohydrate intake to training demands. High training days → higher carb intake. Rest days or low-intensity days → lower carbs. This optimises glycogen availability for hard sessions while managing total calorie intake on easier days.

Pre-competition carb loading: For endurance events over 90 minutes, carb loading (increasing carb intake to 8–10g/kg for 2–3 days before competition while tapering training) supercompensates glycogen stores, delaying fatigue. Not beneficial for strength sports or events under 60–90 minutes.

Intra-workout nutrition: For training sessions over 60–90 minutes, 30–60g carbohydrate per hour (sports drinks, gels, banana) maintains blood glucose and delays glycogen depletion. Glucose + fructose combinations (2:1 ratio) can absorb up to 90g carbohydrate per hour — relevant for ultra-endurance athletes.

Hydration: Even mild dehydration (1–2% body weight) impairs strength, power, and cognitive function. Drink 2–3 litres of water per day baseline. Add 500–750ml per hour of moderate-intensity exercise. Urine colour should be pale yellow (not clear — indicates over-hydration; not dark yellow — indicates under-hydration).

Supplements supplement a good diet — they do not replace it. The supplement industry is worth billions and is largely built on marketing rather than science. 5 Nutrition Rules That Always Work

1. Hit your protein target every day. Everything else is secondary to this.

2. Eat mostly whole, minimally processed foods. If it doesn't have an ingredient list, it's generally a good choice.

3. Eat plenty of vegetables. Fibre, micronutrients, volume without calories. Most people eat nowhere near enough.

4. Drink water first. Aim for 2–3 litres per day baseline. Most "hunger" between meals is dehydration.

5. Make it sustainable. Include the foods you enjoy. An 80% "perfect" diet maintained for a year beats a 100% perfect diet maintained for 3 weeks.

How to Talk to Clients About Nutrition

Start with education, not restriction. Explain why protein matters before telling them to eat more chicken. Understanding drives compliance.

Meet them where they are. A client eating McDonald's twice a day needs simple first steps — not a meal prep plan with six tupperware containers. Progress beats perfection.

Use tracking strategically. For clients who have never tracked, even 2–4 weeks of tracking builds permanent food literacy. It doesn't have to be forever.

Address the social element. Life happens. Restaurants, parties, holidays, and stressful weeks are all real. A plan that accounts for them is a plan that works. Build in flexibility by design.

Focus on what to add, not what to remove. Adding protein to every meal, adding a vegetable to lunch, adding water — these are positive additions. Clients respond better to additions than restrictions psychologically.
